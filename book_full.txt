1.1 Conway's Self-Memory System — Autobiographical Memory Hierarchy
Paper: [P13] Conway, M.A. & Pleydell-Pearce, C.W. (2000). "The Construction of Autobiographical Memories in the Self-Memory System." *Psychological Review*, 107(2), 261–288.
Key Findings:
Human memory is a three-level hierarchy: lifetime periods (identity chapters like "college" or "after dad died") → general events (narrative episodes) → event-specific knowledge (atomic facts and sensory details).
The working self uses current goals to modulate which memories are constructed and retrieved — retrieval is goal-directed, not just similarity-directed.
Memories organize along two axes: temporal (when) and thematic (what kind).
The self and its current goals function as control processes that modulate memory construction.
Morrigan Implementation:
MemoryAtomSchema captures event-specific knowledge (the lowest hierarchy level). Each atom stores: fact, category, importance, valence, temporal.period, temporal.eventDate, temporal.isOngoing.
Molecule synthesis (Step 4 of flush pipeline) clusters 3+ linked atoms into narrative paragraphs — moving from event-specific knowledge up to general events.
`temporal.period` field maps directly to lifetime periods.
`goalAlignsWithEmotion()` implements the working self concept — current emotional goal shapes retrieval.
Status: Partially implemented.
Gaps & Improvements:
Period-based retrieval grouping not implemented. Atoms are injected as a flat list despite Conway showing period organization is core to identity construction. *Fix: Group atoms by temporal.period in buildSystemPrompt() position 8.*
Goal inference never wired. goalState is hardcoded to "neutral", so the working self concept (retrieval shaped by current goals) never activates. The goalAlignsWithEmotion() function exists but the goal inference LLM call was never built. *Now partially addressed by inferGoalStateLLM() with 5s timeout and regex fallback — verify this is active in production.*
No explicit general-event layer. Molecules approximate this, but Conway's model has a structured general-event layer between atoms and periods. Consider a middle tier of episode summaries.

1.2 Park et al.'s Generative Agents — Retrieval Scoring
Paper: [P4] Park, J.S., O'Brien, J.C., Cai, C.J., et al. (2023). "Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior." *UIST '23*. arXiv:2304.03442.
Key Findings:
Triple-weighted retrieval scoring: relevance (cosine similarity), importance (LLM-assigned 1-10), recency (exponential decay).
Reflection threshold mechanism — agents periodically synthesize memories into higher-level reflections.
25 simulated agents maintained believable individual behavior over extended periods using this architecture.
Morrigan Implementation:
`scoreMemory()` computes: similarity × 0.55 + (importance/5) × 0.25 + recencyDecay × 0.10 + valenceBoost × 0.10.
`retrieveTopK()` scores all atoms, sorts descending, returns top K.
Recency uses 90-day exponential decay — intentionally the lightest weight because companion AI values old memories ("you told me about your dad six months ago" is powerful).
Status: Fully implemented with Morrigan-specific extensions.
Improvements from New Research:
[P82] Wang et al. (2025) suggests creating paired retrieval/context memory units — a lightweight unit for matching and a richer unit for injection. This would improve retrieval speed while maintaining injection quality.
[P81] Liu et al. (2025) proposes a memory lifecycle (Formation → Evolution → Retrieval) that distinguishes factual, experiential, and working memory. Current atoms don't differentiate between "user works at X" (factual) and "user told me a story about childhood" (experiential) — consider adding a memoryType field.

1.3 Damasio's Somatic Marker Hypothesis — Emotional Memory Encoding
Paper: [P14] Damasio, A.R. (1994). *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain.* See also: Damasio (1996). "The somatic marker hypothesis and the possible functions of the prefrontal cortex." *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. Lond. B*, 351, 1413–1420.
Key Findings:
Normal decision-making begins with an emotional signal (a "somatic marker" or "gut feeling") before conscious reasoning engages.
Patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage can reason perfectly but cannot decide — they lack the emotional signal that precedes rational thought.
Emotional memories encode deeper and retrieve faster than neutral ones.
Morrigan Implementation (two applications):
Emotional Priming (System Prompt Position 5): A parallel LLM call (SOMATIC_MARKER_PROMPT) generates Morrigan's "gut feeling" before main response generation. ~80 tokens, temp 0.1, 5s timeout. Injected as primingSentence in buildSystemPrompt(). Skipped during crisis mode.
Valence tracking on memory atoms: Separate valence.emotion and valence.charge fields. Emotional memories get retrieval boost via goalAlignsWithEmotion().
Status: Somatic marker now implemented. Valence-based retrieval partially active.
New Validation:
[P87] Zhang et al. (2025) provides neural-network-level evidence that emotional signals persist in LLM hidden layers for hundreds of subsequent tokens. This means the somatic marker sentence at position 5 genuinely affects the emotional coloring of the entire response — it's not just a prompt engineering trick.
[P88] Song et al. (2025) identified specific "emotion circuits" in LLMs. Direct circuit modulation achieves 99.65% emotion-expression accuracy vs. 67.71% for prompt-based steering on some emotions. Implication: simple, direct emotional labels ("I feel protective") may be more effective in the somatic marker than elaborate descriptions.

1.4 Bruner's Narrative Theory — Contradictions as Identity Data
Paper: [P15] Bruner, J.S. (1990). *Acts of Meaning.* Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press. Bruner, J. (1991). "The Narrative Construction of Reality." *Critical Inquiry*, 18(1), 1–21.
Key Findings:
Humans construct identity through narrative, and narratives inherently contain contradictions.
"I hate my dad" and "I miss him" are both true simultaneously.
There is no essential self — identity is continuously constructed and reconstructed.
Contradictions are not errors to resolve but data about the complexity of human experience.
Morrigan Implementation:
Both contradicting atoms are preserved (never delete either).
Three-gate detection: cosine similarity > 0.78, valence diff > 0.5, LLM confirmation.
Tension pairs injected into system prompt for Morrigan to reference naturally.
Contradiction system overhaul (v2.6): 5-category classifier (TEMPORAL_EVOLUTION, AMBIVALENCE, GENUINE_CONTRADICTION, REFINEMENT, NOT_CONTRADICTORY) with bidirectional pair dedup and lifecycle filtering.
Status: Fully implemented with sophisticated classification.

1.6 Knowledge Conflicts — Temporal vs. Genuine Contradictions

Lifecycle filtering resolves evolved contradictions.

1.12 NEW: Multiple Memory Systems — Episodic vs. Semantic
Paper: [P82] Wang, T., et al. (2025). "Multiple Memory Systems for Enhancing the Long-term Memory of Agent." arXiv:2508.15294.
Key Findings:
Inspired by Tulving's memory system classification.
Extracts episodic memory (what happened), semantic memory (facts/preferences), and cognitive perspectives (beliefs/mental models).
Paired "retrieval memory units" and "contextual memory units" with 1:1 correspondence.
Outperforms MemoryBank and A-MEM in retrieval quality.
Gap in Morrigan:
All memories stored as undifferentiated MemoryAtom objects. No distinction between "user works at a hospital" (semantic) and "user told me a long story about their first day at work, visibly emotional" (episodic).
Recommendation: Add memoryType field to MemoryAtomSchema: "semantic" (facts, preferences), "episodic" (narrative events, disclosed experiences), "cognitive" (beliefs, attitudes, mental models). Use type-aware retrieval: emotional exchanges should pull more episodic memories, factual questions should pull more semantic memories.

1.13 NEW: Memory Lifecycle Framework
Paper: [P81] Liu, S., et al. (2025). "Memory in the Age of AI Agents: A Survey." arXiv:2512.13564.
Key Findings:
Unified framework: three forms (token-level, parametric, latent), three functions (factual, experiential, working), lifecycle of Formation → Evolution → Retrieval.
Evolution includes: consolidation (merging/generalizing), updating (revising conflicts), and forgetting (removing outdated info).
Gap in Morrigan:
Molecule synthesis handles consolidation. Contradiction system handles updating. Ebbinghaus decay handles forgetting. But these operate independently.
Recommendation: Implement a unified memory evolution pass during session finalization that coordinates all three operations: consolidate (molecule synthesis), update (contradiction resolution), and forget (decay pruning) in a single lifecycle step.

1.14 NEW: Knowledge Graph Memory
Paper: [P83] Gao, Y., et al. (2025). "Memoria: A Scalable Agentic Memory Framework for Personalized Conversational AI." arXiv:2512.12686.
Key Findings:
Combines dynamic session-level summarization with a weighted knowledge graph for user modeling.
Incrementally captures user traits, preferences, and behavioral patterns as structured entities and relationships.
87.1% accuracy while reducing prompt tokens from 115K to under 400.
Gap in Morrigan:
Memory retrieval relies on cosine similarity on flat embeddings. A graph structure would enable relational queries ("find memories related to the user's father") by traversing relationship edges.
Recommendation: Long-term: evolve molecule synthesis toward a knowledge graph where atoms are nodes and relationships (temporal, causal, thematic) are typed edges.

2.1 Altman & Taylor — Social Penetration Theory
Paper: [P12] Altman, I. & Taylor, D.A. (1973). *Social Penetration: The Development of Interpersonal Relationships.* Holt, Rinehart & Winston.
Key Findings:
Relationships develop through progressive, reciprocal self-disclosure.
Four stages: orientation (surface), exploratory affective (values/attitudes), affective (personal feelings), stable (deep vulnerability).
Disclosure depth explains 40–60% of relationship satisfaction variance.
Depth and breadth are independent dimensions — you can go deep in one topic while staying surface in others.
Morrigan Implementation:
`sptDepth` (1–4): Deepest emotional disclosure level reached. Never decrements.
`sptBreadth` (Map): Per-topic depth map (e.g., { family: 2, work: 1, identity: 3 }).
Depth-gated self-disclosure: Morrigan cannot share depth-3+ self-atoms until user reaches that depth.
SPT Note at system prompt position 4 with anti-excessive-empathy instruction.
Status: Fully implemented — foundational to entire architecture.

2.2 Aron's Fast Friends Protocol — Graduated Closeness
Paper: [P56] Aron, A., et al. (1997). "The Experimental Generation of Interpersonal Closeness." *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 23(4).
Key Findings:
36 questions with graduated intimacy depth reliably generated closeness between strangers in 45 minutes.
Back-and-forth reciprocal disclosure that increases gradually is optimal — sharing too much too fast backfires.
Three mechanisms: showing interest (asking), enabling revelation (content), responding to what's revealed (reception).
Aron's three question sets map directly to SPT depth levels: Set 1 (surface preferences, Q1-12) = depth 1 atoms open; Set 2 (values/memories, Q13-24) = depth 1-2 atoms; Set 3 (vulnerability/identity, Q25-36) = depth 3-4 gate. Order must be preserved -- advancing to Set 3 topics before Set 1 rapport causes withdrawal, which is the empirical basis for SPT's non-decrement rule.
Morrigan Implementation:
Aron Question Set
SPT Depth
Morrigan Gate
Set 1 (Q1–12): "Who would be your ideal dinner guest?"
Depth 1: Surface facts
Open — all depth-1 self-atoms eligible
Set 2 (Q13–24): "What is your most treasured memory?"
Depth 2: Values/memories
Light gate — depth ≤ 2 atoms
Set 2–3 boundary: "How close is your family?"
Depth 2–3 transition
Confidence-gated advancement
Set 3 (Q25–36): "Share a personal problem"
Depth 3–4: Vulnerability
Hard gate — reception override

Critical insight: Questions MUST proceed in order. Asking Q33 ("Whose death would affect you most?") before establishing Set 1 rapport causes withdrawal. This is the empirical basis for the non-decrement rule.
Status: Fully implemented.

Paper: [P58] Multiple research groups (2024). "AI Outperforms Humans in Establishing Interpersonal Closeness in Deep-Talk Interactions." Double-blind RCT, N=492.
Key Findings:
When labelled as human, AI outperformed actual human partners in establishing closeness.

3.1 Liu et al. — Inner Thoughts Framework (Primary Reference)
Paper: [P1] Liu, X.B., et al. (2025). "Proactive Conversational Agents with Inner Thoughts." *CHI 2025, Yokohama*. arXiv:2501.00383.
Key Findings:
Five-stage pipeline: trigger → retrieval → thought formation → evaluation → participation.
G-Eval weighted token probability scoring for motivation (adapted to 0–10 scale).
Dual positive/negative reasoning reduces score overrating.
Cadence damping: once every 3–4 messages.
User study (n=24): 82% preference over reactive baselines.
Morrigan Implementation (five stages):
Stage 1 — Trigger: shouldTriggerThoughtFormation() checks cadence (3+ messages since last injection), active callbacks, available self-atoms, message length.
Stage 2 — Retrieval: gatherThoughtMaterial() assembles: top 3 eligible self-atoms, top 2 unconsumed callbacks, selfReflectionState, relationship narrative, trust level, sptDepth. +atRisk mode gathers 4 callbacks instead of 2.
Stage 3 — Formation: INNER_THOUGHT_FORMATION_PROMPT() with four steps:
Theory of Mind assessment (per [P10])
Generate 2–3 candidate thoughts (typed: callback, disclosure, reaction, concern, withholding)
Score each thought on 4 sub-dimensions (relevance, personalGain, trustGate, timing) = 0–10 total
Participation directive — behavioral instruction for highest-scoring thought
Stage 4 — Evaluate & Select: Post-LLM depth gate validation, thought evolution handling, age penalty (0.25/message), silence bonus, threshold check.
Thought block format: "[INNER THOUGHT -- {type}] Theory of Mind: {tom}. Directive: {participationDirective}." The main LLM receives only the winning thought's behavioral directive -- not the raw candidate texts or the intermediate scores. The two candidate thoughts evaluated but not selected are never injected. Only the derived directive from the winning candidate enters the system prompt at Position 6.
Threshold: 4.0 (at-risk: 3.5), calibrated against [P70] XiaoIce's finding that over-strict thresholds produce long-term CPS decay. Initial implementation at 7.0 was too strict -- most thoughts never fired. Historical calibration detail is in CLAUDE.md change log.
Status: Fully implemented — the system's most significant technical achievement.

5. Technical Foundation

This section answers the conceptual questions that arise when implementing the system: what is an embedding, exactly? why does prompt position matter mechanically? why does cosine similarity work for memory retrieval? These questions are not abstract — getting them wrong leads to incorrect architectural reasoning.

5.1 Two Types of Embeddings

Two entirely separate embedding systems operate in Morrigan. Confusing them leads to incorrect architectural reasoning.

TYPE A — Memory Embeddings (text-embedding-3-small API):
External API call (embedText()). Encodes entire sentences into 1536-dim vectors for cosine similarity retrieval. text-embedding-3-small maps semantic meaning to geometric position — "He mentioned losing his job" and "He said he was unemployed" have cosine ~0.95. Used for MemoryAtom.embedding[], retrieveTopK(), reRankWithLLM(). Your application code only ever works with Type A.

TYPE B — Token Embeddings (internal to the LLM transformer):
Inside the LLM. nn.Embedding lookup table — feed it token ID 3472, get back row 3472. No computation, just a table read. Start as random numbers, become meaningful through training on billions of tokens. Morrigan's code never touches these. They are only accessible by the LLM itself during response generation.

THE DISTINCTION: embedText(fact) = Type A (your code, semantic retrieval). LLM processing the prompt internally = Type B (invisible to your code, inside the transformer). The memory retrieval pipeline uses Type A. Response generation uses Type B internally.

5.2 What Happens Mechanically When the 13-Layer Prompt Enters the LLM

The LLM processes the prompt as follows: tokenize text into subword tokens (BPE, fixed pre-trained table, not neural) → look up each token in an internal embedding table → add positional vectors (second separate lookup table, one vector per position, added element-wise to preserve dimensionality) → run 32 attention+FFN blocks that progressively refine the token vectors → project the final token vector to vocabulary logits → select next token → repeat from scratch. Each attention block runs 32 heads in parallel, each head learning to track different patterns (syntax, coreference, emotional register, topic). The shape [sequence_length, embed_dim] never changes; only the meaning packed into those vectors deepens.

Why MongoDB Exists:
The LLM has NO persistent state between API calls. Every call is completely stateless — no memory of previous conversations, no carry-over activations, no internal representation of Morrigan's relationship with the user. This is not a limitation that can be patched inside the model. MongoDB is the solution: it stores the relationship state (memories, trust level, feelings, milestones, self-atom disclosures) and injects it into the prompt context on every call. Without the database, each conversation would start from zero.

5.3 Why Prompt Position and Content Order Matter

Attention is position-aware via positional embeddings. Tokens early in the sequence influence later ones because later tokens attend back to earlier tokens during their Query/Key computation. The 13-layer ordering is not arbitrary. (See Part II Section 3 for the full layer table with research basis per position.)

Mechanically: when the character definition (Position 2, ~3,200 tokens) appears before everything else, by the time the model processes Position 5 (somatic marker, ~30 tokens), the character vectors have already been attended to hundreds of times. The emotional priming sentence at Position 5 activates into an already-character-shaped context, producing a different (measurably more on-character) response than if the same sentence appeared first.

Directive format rationale: Instructions formatted as declarative states ("You are feeling a pull toward honesty") outperform imperative commands ("You must be honest") because transformer attention treats them as contextual facts to integrate rather than rule triggers, which creates conflict with the character definition at Position 2.

5.4 Why Cosine Similarity Retrieval Works

text-embedding-3-small is trained to map semantic meaning to geometric position in a 1536-dimensional space. Similar meanings = nearby positions = high cosine similarity. This is not fuzzy keyword matching — it is learned geometric structure. "He mentioned losing his job" and "He said he was unemployed" are geometrically close not because they share words but because the model was trained on millions of examples that use both phrasings in similar contexts.

The retrieval formula (similarity x 0.55 + importance x 0.25 + recency x 0.10 + valence x 0.10) is intentionally similarity-heavy. Weighting rationale is in Section 1.2. Note: cosine similarity scoring is Stage 1 of a two-stage pipeline — Stage 2 is LLM-based cross-encoder re-ranking (reRankWithLLM()). See Section 1.10 [P17] for rationale. The combined pipeline retrieves cosine top-2k then re-ranks to final top-k.

5.5 Multi-Head Attention and the Inner Thought System

LLaMA 8B runs 32 attention heads per block (across all 32 transformer layers). Each head learns to track different relationship patterns simultaneously. Research has identified heads specializing in: grammatical subject-verb binding, coreference resolution (tracking what "he" refers to across long context), emotional register detection, topical continuity, and persona consistency.

For the research basis on emotion circuits and why direct emotional labels outperform elaborate descriptions, see Sections 4.7 [P88] and 4.8 [P87]. The architecture implication: the specialization of certain attention heads for emotional content is why the somatic marker at Position 5 produces measurable effects on response emotional register — it is not a prompt engineering trick but a mechanistic intervention.

The inner thought system exploits head specialization directly. By injecting the inner thought block at Position 6 (immediately after the somatic marker at 5), the thought material is processed by the same heads that are tracking emotional register from Position 5. This is the mechanistic reason for the ordering — not intuition, but the documented behavior of attention head specialization in transformer models.
